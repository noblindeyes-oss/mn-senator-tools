Mapping the Distance Between People and Care — SD-3 Data Tool for Rural Health Advocacy
Senator Hauschild came to the legislature from healthcare philanthropy — from the work of making the case for communities that distance leaves behind. SD-3 has some of the most striking healthcare access data in Minnesota. This tool was built to make that data visible.
Northeast Minnesota has some of the longest distances to healthcare in the nation. Grand Portage is 145 miles from the nearest trauma center. Cook County's only hospital stopped delivering babies because the patient volume is too small to maintain the clinical skills needed. This tool maps what those distances mean, explains why rural hospitals struggle to stay solvent, and generates data-backed briefs for the people trying to change it.

Understanding the Critical Access Hospital (CAH) Model

  • CAH Designation: ≤25 beds, rural location, ≤35 miles from next nearest hospital
  • Medicare Reimbursement: 101% of "reasonable costs" (vs. DRG flat payment at standard hospitals)
  • The Benefit: Cost-based reimbursement should theoretically cover operating costs
  • The Problem: When patient volume is tiny and the share of Medicaid/uninsured patients is high, even 101% cost coverage does not produce the operating margin needed for capital investment

The Reinforcing Failure Cycle — Financial Pressure on Critical Access Hospitals

The Vicious Cycle in Plain Language

A hospital serving 5,500 people cannot generate enough patient volume to cover its fixed costs. It cannot attract specialists because the volume is not there to support them. When it cannot attract staff, it pays agency nurses at 30–50% premiums, further eroding margins. When margins disappear, capital investment stops. Equipment ages. Services are cut — OB deliveries first, because malpractice premiums require volume the community cannot produce. Each service cut makes the community less viable for families, further reducing the population, further reducing volume. This is the cycle that federal and state policy must break.

Related: Senator Hauschild authored SF1617 (2025) for Cook County Schools construction tax exemption — recognizing that school and hospital viability are linked. Families will not move to or stay in a community that lacks both.

Key Legislative Context: Minnesota's 2023 telehealth parity law requires insurers to reimburse telehealth visits at the same rate as in-person visits — a genuine positive development for rural access. However, broadband infrastructure gaps remain a barrier for many residents in SD-3.
The Broadband-Healthcare Nexus:

For communities like Grand Portage (145 miles from Duluth), telehealth is not a convenience — it is a lifeline. Mental health counseling, primary care follow-ups, dermatology, and psychiatry can all be delivered effectively via video telehealth. But without broadband infrastructure, the 2023 parity law is a promise the infrastructure cannot keep. Interior Cook County and sections of the North Shore corridor remain below the 25 Mbps threshold required for reliable video visits.
