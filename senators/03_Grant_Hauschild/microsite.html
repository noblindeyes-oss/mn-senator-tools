The Legislation — SF1617

A Tax Break Written for One School District.

Cook County Schools sits at the end of the road. Highway 61 terminates in Grand Marais. Construction materials cost more to get there. Contractors cost more to bring there. Every dollar the district pays in sales tax on building supplies is a dollar that does not go to students.

SF1617 creates a refundable sales-tax exemption on construction materials used in a defined package of Cook County Schools upgrades. The district gets back what it pays in sales tax on materials for these specific projects. The exemption is written around a named package of work.[1]

Hauschild chief-authored the bill. He represents Cook County. The connection between the legislation and the constituency is direct and verifiable.

Why This Approach Fits District 3

Senate District 3 covers Cook, Lake, Itasca, Koochiching, and St. Louis counties. The district is geographically larger than nine U.S. states. It is served by a thin network of public schools. When a school district loses a building, there is no nearby alternative.[2]

Construction costs in remote districts carry a distance penalty that metro-area schools do not face. Materials cost more to ship. Contractors cost more to bring in. A refundable sales-tax exemption on building materials is one of the few tools the Legislature can use to directly offset that penalty without requiring a local levy vote.

Hauschild sits on the Education Policy and Taxes committees. Both tracks matter for a bill like this: SF1617 is an education bill that lives in the tax code.[2]

The bill is district-specific legislation from a first-term senator. That is the kind of work that rarely makes headlines but directly serves a constituency. Hauschild authored it because Cook County is in his district and the school district asked for help with the construction-cost burden.

In 2025, Grant Hauschild voted to kill a bill banning local-government NDAs with data center developers. At the same time, officials in his own district had already signed NDAs with Google to keep a $650M data center secret from residents. When the backlash came, Hauschild introduced his own NDA ban. On Record
The Sequence — How It Unfolded
2025 — The Vote
Hauschild Helps Kill NDA Transparency Bill
Sen. Maye Quade introduced a bill to ban local governments from signing non-disclosure agreements with data center developers. The bill needed DFL support to pass. Hauschild was one of five Democrats who voted against it. The bill died.[3]

His stated reason: it had not been properly vetted by committee.
Vote: Against transparency
Concurrent — The Secret in His District
Hermantown Officials Had Already Signed NDAs with Google
While Hauschild was voting against NDA transparency in St. Paul, officials in Hermantown — a city in his own Senate District 3 — had already signed NDAs with Google for a massive data center project known as "Project Loon." The project: 1.8 million square feet on 200+ acres. Estimated cost: $650 million. County commissioners signed non-disclosure agreements that kept the project secret from Hermantown residents.[4][5]
His district. His constituents. Kept in the dark.
July 2025 — The Dodge
"Speaking in Hypotheticals"
When constituents confronted Hauschild about the Hermantown data center, he told a local reporter he was "speaking in hypotheticals." He claimed he "did not know the details of the Hermantown project or how NDAs were being used locally."[5]
"I did not know the details of the Hermantown project or how NDAs were being used locally." Sen. Grant Hauschild, July 2025 · Ref. [5]
March 2026 — The Flip
Hauschild Introduces His Own NDA Ban — a Narrower One
After massive grassroots backlash — the "Stop the Hermantown Data Center" movement — Hauschild introduced SF4548, his own NDA ban bill. But it is narrower than the one he helped kill. His version covers only data centers and only elected officials. The original bill he voted against was a broader ban on local-government NDAs with data center developers.[6]
Narrower than what he killed
Background — The Environmental Connection
SF570: He Also Narrowed Who Can Demand Environmental Review
Hauschild authored SF570 in 2025. The bill rewrote the Environmental Assessment Worksheet petition threshold. Previously, any 100 Minnesota residents could petition for environmental review of a proposed project. Under SF570, those 100 people had to live or own property in the project county or an adjacent county. Similar provisions later appeared in First Special Session SF3.[7][8]

That means the very tool constituents would use to challenge a data center's environmental impact in Hauschild's district has been narrowed — by a bill Hauschild authored.

The District 3 Problem

Senate District 3 includes Lake Superior shoreline, borders the Boundary Waters Canoe Area Wilderness, and contains the Iron Range. Its voters include communities that have fought against industrial permitting on water and wetlands for decades.

When a $650M data center was planned for Hermantown, residents had no say because county officials signed NDAs. Hauschild voted against the tool that would have prevented those NDAs. He then authored a bill that narrowed the environmental review process those same residents would need to challenge the project's impact.[4][7]

The pattern: block transparency, dodge accountability, then reposition as a reformer when the political cost arrives.
