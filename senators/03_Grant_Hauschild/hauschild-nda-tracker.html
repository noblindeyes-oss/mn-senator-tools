An interactive record of how Senator Grant Hauschild voted to kill NDA transparency, stayed silent while his own constituents were kept in the dark by those same NDAs, and then rebranded himself as a transparency champion with a narrower bill. This tool assembles the public record.

January 2025
Hauschild Authors SF570
Env. Review

Hauschild introduces SF570, rewriting the Environmental Assessment Worksheet petition threshold. Previously, any 100 Minnesota residents could petition for environmental review of a proposed project. SF570 limits petitioners to those who live or own property in the project county or an adjacent county.[1]

This narrows the pool of people who can trigger environmental review of large projects, including data centers.

SF570, 94th Legislature, as introduced Jan. 23, 2025. Revisor.mn.gov

2025 Session
Maye Quade's NDA Ban Comes to a Vote
Senate Vote

Sen. Maye Quade introduces a bill to ban local governments from signing non-disclosure agreements with data center developers. The bill needs DFL votes to pass.

Hauschild is one of five Democrats who vote against it. The bill dies.[2]

His stated objection: the bill had not been properly vetted by committee.

Senate vote record, 2025.

↓ Meanwhile, in Hauschild's District ↓
Concurrent — 2025
Hermantown Signs NDAs with Google
NDA Signed

Officials in Hermantown, a city in Senate District 3, sign non-disclosure agreements with Google for a massive data center project code-named "Project Loon."[3]

The project: approximately 1.8 million square feet on 200+ acres. Estimated cost: roughly $650 million. St. Louis County commissioners sign NDAs keeping the project secret from residents.

Hermantown residents have no knowledge of the project. Their elected officials are bound by the very type of NDA that Hauschild just voted to keep legal.

Local news reporting on Project Loon, Hermantown.

2025
SF570 Provisions Enacted via First Special Session SF3
Enacted

SF570 does not become law as a standalone bill. However, the environmental review changes Hauschild proposed — including the EAW petition residency restriction and feedlot permit review sequencing — appear in First Special Session SF3, which is enacted as Session Law Chapter 1.[4][5]

The policy survives even though the vehicle changed.

First Special Session SF3, Session Law Ch. 1. Revisor.mn.gov

July 2025
Constituents Confront Hauschild
Public Backlash

As details of the Hermantown data center become public, constituents confront Hauschild directly. In a local news interview, he responds:

"I did not know the details of the Hermantown project or how NDAs were being used locally." Sen. Grant Hauschild · July 2025

A reporter notes he was "speaking in hypotheticals" about the NDA issue. The claim: the senator who voted to keep NDAs legal was unaware that NDAs were being used in his own district.[6]

Local news interview, July 2025.

"Stop the Hermantown Data Center" Movement
Grassroots

Residents organize under the banner "Stop the Hermantown Data Center." The movement gains traction across District 3 and statewide. Concerns include water usage, energy consumption, noise, and the use of NDAs to bypass public input.[6]

The political pressure on Hauschild intensifies.

March 2026
Hauschild Introduces SF4548 — His Own NDA Ban
New Bill

After months of constituent pressure, Hauschild introduces SF4548 — an NDA ban bill. But it is narrower than the one he helped kill:[7]

Key differences:

Maye Quade's original bill: banned local governments from signing NDAs with data center developers (broad scope).

Hauschild's SF4548: bans NDAs only for data centers and only when signed by elected officials. Staff and appointed officials may still be covered differently.

He rebranded opposition as leadership. The question is whether the narrower version was a genuine policy refinement or a political calculation.

The Gap

Hauschild killed the broader bill on procedural grounds, then introduced a narrower version only after constituents organized against the exact type of NDA secrecy the original bill would have prevented. The replacement covers less ground. The timeline suggests the reversal was driven by political pressure, not policy conviction.

SF570 changed who can petition for environmental review of large projects. Here is what changed and why it matters in the context of the Hermantown data center.

Before SF570 / SF3

Any 100 Minnesota residents could petition for a discretionary Environmental Assessment Worksheet (EAW) on a proposed project, regardless of where they lived in the state.
This meant statewide environmental organizations could anchor petitions for projects anywhere in Minnesota.
→

After SF570 / SF3 Provisions

Those 100 petitioners must live or own property in the project county or an immediately adjacent county. Out-of-area residents can no longer anchor petitions.[1][4]
This restricts the geographic pool of eligible petitioners, making it harder for statewide groups to trigger environmental review of local projects.

Why This Matters for the Data Center

Narrowed Petitioner Pool

Statewide environmental groups can no longer independently anchor 100-person EAW petitions for projects in Hermantown or St. Louis County. The petition pool is now geographically restricted.

Hauschild Authored Both

The same senator who voted to keep data center NDAs legal also authored the bill that restricts who can demand environmental review of data center projects in his district.

The Double Bind

Residents were kept in the dark by NDAs (which Hauschild voted to keep legal), and the process they would use to challenge the project's environmental impact was narrowed (by a bill Hauschild authored).
