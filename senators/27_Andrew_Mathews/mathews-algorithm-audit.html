Module 2 — Family Account Audit Checklist
Section A — Parental Consent
SF1528 requires verifiable parental consent before account creation for users under 18.
[?] What to look for
Look for a date-of-birth field or age gate during the sign-up process. A simple "Are you 13+" checkbox is weaker than a full date-of-birth entry.
[?] What to look for
Verifiable consent means more than a checkbox. Look for: email sent to a parent, credit card micro-transaction, government ID verification, or a video call. If the child could complete sign-up without any parent involvement, this is likely not met.
[?] What to look for
Check your email, text messages, or app notifications for a consent request from the platform. If you never received anything and your child has an active account, the platform may not have sought verifiable consent.
Section B — Algorithmic Feed (Core Prohibition)
SF1528 prohibits platforms from using recommendation algorithms to push user-generated content at Minnesota minors. Permitted: chronological feeds, content from followed accounts, and search results.
[?] What to look for
Scroll through your child's main feed. If posts appear from accounts your child has never followed or interacted with, those are algorithmically recommended. Look for labels like "Suggested for you" or "Based on your interests."
[?] What to look for
Content that appears without any search or follow action from your child is likely algorithmically pushed. This includes "Explore," "Discover," or "For You" pages that populate automatically.
[?] What to look for
If the platform has a separate "Following" feed and a "For You" feed, the "For You" feed is algorithmic. Check if the default view when opening the app is the algorithmic feed.
[?] What to look for
Check the platform's settings for a "chronological" or "latest" feed option. Some platforms offer this but make it hard to find or default back to the algorithmic feed. If no such option exists, check this box.
Section C — Permitted Exceptions
These exceptions may apply. If YES, the platform may be within the law's allowed activity.
[?] What to look for
If you set up parental controls or content filters on the platform and the content your child sees is shaped by those settings, this may be an exception under SF1528.
[?] What to look for
Content from verified government agencies, schools, or educational institutions may be exempt. Check if the recommended content comes from such official accounts.

Module 4 — What the Law Says (Plain Language)

      
SF1528 — Stop Online Targeting Against Kids Act (SOTA Kids Act)


      

        Chief author: Senator Andrew Mathews (SD-27). Co-authors: Senators Gruenhagen and Lucero. Companion: HF 48.
        Introduced February 17, 2025 — 94th Minnesota Legislature. Referred to Senate Commerce and Consumer Protection Committee.
      


      

        

          1. What SF1528 Covers
          

            This law applies to large social media platforms operating in Minnesota — specifically those with more than 1 million global account holders. It says they cannot use recommendation algorithms to push content at your child: content your child didn't ask for, didn't search for, and doesn't come from accounts your child chose to follow.
          

        

      


      

        

          2. What Platforms Are Still Allowed to Do
          

            Platforms can still show your child content from accounts they follow. They can still show chronological feeds. They can still show search results. What they cannot do is use an algorithm to push additional, unsolicited content at a minor.
          

        

      


      

        

          3. The Consent Requirement
          

            Before your child (under 18) creates an account on a covered platform, the platform must get verifiable parental consent — not just a checkbox. This means the platform must have a way to confirm a parent actually approved the account creation.
          

        

      


      

        

          4. What the Penalties Are
          

            $1,000 per violation. Up to $100,000 per user per year. The parent or guardian can bring a civil claim under the statute.
          

        

      


      

        

          5. When This Takes Effect
          

            SF1528 is proposed to take effect January 1, 2026, if signed into law. As of its introduction in February 2025, the bill is in the Senate Commerce and Consumer Protection Committee.
          

        

      


      

        

          6. What This Law Does Not Do
          

            This law does not ban social media for minors. It does not require platforms to verify every user's age. It focuses specifically on the algorithm — the system that decides what to push at your child beyond what they asked for.
