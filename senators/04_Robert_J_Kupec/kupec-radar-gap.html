Minnesota Radar Gap Explorer

Understanding the weather visibility problem that Sen. Rob Kupec has spent a career studying and a legislative session trying to fix.
SD-4 • SF580 • 2025
Rob Kupec spent nearly a decade as chief meteorologist at KVRR-TV watching storms develop over a part of Minnesota where NWS radar can't see below 10,000 feet. In 2025, he introduced SF580 -- a $3.27 million bill to fix that. The bill passed committee with bipartisan support. It did not make it to the floor. The gap is still there. This tool shows you what the gap looks like, how it forms, what it would cost to fix, and why a meteorologist turned state senator keeps pushing on it.

Meteorologist's note: The earth curves away from the radar beam at a rate of about 1.2 miles per 100 miles of distance. Even a 0.5-degree tilt means the beam rises roughly 3,000 feet every 50 miles. By the time the beam reaches the center of western Minnesota from the nearest NWS radar, it cannot see anything below 10,000 feet -- where most severe weather, including tornadoes, actually forms.

■ Storm Scenarios -- What the Gap Means

Severe weather forms below 10,000 feet. That is where tornadoes develop, where hail forms, where the rotational signatures appear that trigger warnings. In the gap zone, NWS radar cannot see any of it. These scenarios illustrate what happens when storms enter the blind spot.

Scenario: Supercell Enters Gap

Based on typical June severe pattern

A supercell thunderstorm tracks northeast from South Dakota into Grant County. KMVX (Mayville) can see the storm's upper structure above 11,000 ft. It cannot see the mesocyclone at 3,000 ft where tornado rotation develops.

NWS forecasters lose the hook echo signature. The tornado warning that would normally issue with 15 minutes of lead time does not go out until the funnel is spotted visually.

RESULT: Warning lead time drops from ~15 min to ~0 min. Rural residents have no siren and no alert.

Scenario: Derecho Crosses Western MN

Based on 2022 SD/MN derecho pattern

A fast-moving derecho (straight-line wind event) crosses from the Dakotas into Minnesota. Wind speeds exceed 80 mph at ground level. The bow echo structure is partially visible at upper levels, but the low-level jet driving the destruction is below 6,000 ft.

Without low-level radar data, the NWS cannot accurately estimate wind speeds or confirm the bow echo's ground-level intensity across 8 counties.

RESULT: NWS issues generic severe thunderstorm warning instead of the specific "destructive winds" warning that triggers emergency alerts on cell phones.

Scenario: Flash Flood in Becker County

Based on training thunderstorm pattern

Multiple thunderstorm cells train over the same area near Detroit Lakes. Rainfall rates exceed 2 inches per hour. Because radar can only see the upper portions of the storms, the NWS precipitation estimate algorithm underestimates rainfall by 40-60%.

Flash flood warnings are delayed. Roads flood before alerts arrive. The Detroit Lakes area has no early warning.

RESULT: Rainfall underestimated by ~50%. Flash flood warning issues 30-45 min late.
2+in/hr actual rainfall
~50%underestimate by radar

The Fix: With SF580 Radars

Projected improvement

With 8 Climavision X-band radars deployed per SF580, each covering a 60-mile radius down to ~60 ft above ground level, the NWS and local forecasters gain the low-level data they need.

Tornado rotation signatures visible at 3,000 ft. Flash flood rainfall estimates accurate within 15%. Derecho wind structures tracked in real time. Warning lead times return to national averages.

RESULT: Warning lead times restored. Population protection equivalent to the rest of Minnesota. Cost: ~$1.33 per resident.

Why the Bill Stalled

SF580 passed the Agriculture, Broadband, and Rural Development Committee in March 2025 with bipartisan support (co-authored by Sen. Andrew Lang, R-Olivia). Sen. Kupec attributed the bill's failure to advance to the Senate judiciary committee's need to prioritize prison and court funding during a tight budget year -- a scheduling conflict, not opposition to the radar concept. The issue remains unfinished.
