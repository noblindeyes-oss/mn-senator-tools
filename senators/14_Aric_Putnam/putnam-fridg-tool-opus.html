Program Background

The Food Retail Improvement and Development Grant (FRIDG) Program was created through legislation authored by Sen. Aric Putnam (SD-14) as a successor to the Good Food Access Program. The key change: the new program added clearer eligibility criteria, including explicit use of USDA food access designations to define "underserved communities," ensuring that grants reach stores in areas with documented food access gaps rather than simply stores that apply.

MDA administers the program through competitive grant rounds typically held each fiscal year.

"Does My Store Qualify?"

Minnesota's Food Retail Improvement and Development Grant (FRIDG) program funds equipment and building improvements for small food retailers serving underserved communities — corner stores, farmers' markets, mobile markets, food hubs, and small groceries. Grants range from $5,000 to $75,000, with a 25% match required. This tool helps store owners and community organizations find out whether they qualify, what they need to apply, and how to put their application narrative together.

The FRIDG program rewrote the rules for how Minnesota directs food access grants — adding clearer eligibility criteria so that money reaches stores in communities with documented gaps. This tool is designed to help store owners in those communities figure out whether they qualify and how to apply.

Section B

Community Served

Your store must serve an underserved community. There are three qualifying pathways — you may meet one or more. Select all that apply.

Pathway 1: Low-Income / Low-Access Census Tract

Your store must be located in, or primarily serve residents of, a census tract that is (a) low-income AND (b) low-access to supermarkets. USDA defines low-income as a census tract where the poverty rate is at or above 20%, or where median family income does not exceed 80% of the statewide median. Low-access means a significant share of the tract's population lives more than 1 mile from the nearest supermarket (urban) or more than 10 miles (rural).

How to check your census tract:
Visit the USDA Food Access Research Atlas and enter your store address. Look for tracts highlighted as both "low-income" and "low-access."

The Minnesota Department of Agriculture also publishes an eligible-areas map for the FRIDG program on the MDA FRIDG page.

Pathway 2: Underserved Population (Non-Geographic)

Even if your census tract does not meet the USDA low-income/low-access standard, you may qualify if you primarily serve a population with unmet food access needs.

Pathway 3: Rural / Frontier Area

In rural Minnesota, a store that is the sole food retail option for a large geographic area may qualify under the FRIDG underserved-community standard even if census tract data is incomplete.

Section C

SNAP Authorization

SNAP (EBT) authorization is required for the Retailer Equipment and Infrastructure grant track. It is not required for the Technical Assistance track.

SNAP authorization is required for equipment/infrastructure grants but not for technical assistance grants. If you are not yet SNAP-authorized, you can apply through the USDA SNAP Retailer Application. MDA may accept applications with pending SNAP authorization if you can provide documentation that the process is underway. This will be flagged as an action item on your checklist.
