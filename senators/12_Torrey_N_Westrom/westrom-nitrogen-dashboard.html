B — UMN-Morris Green Ammonia Project

What is green ammonia? Ammonia (NH₃) synthesized from air (N₂) and water (H₂O) using electrolysis and the Haber-Bosch process, powered by renewable electricity instead of natural gas.

The UMN-Morris facility is a second-generation plant, approximately 17× the size of the pilot it replaced, opened in 2025 in Stevens County — within or adjacent to SD-12. It demonstrates that modular green ammonia production is technically viable at a scale relevant to cooperatives.

Why it matters locally: It breaks the link between nitrogen fertilizer cost and natural gas prices. Western Minnesota's wind belt can supply off-peak power, and co-op ownership models keep production economics local.

Senator Westrom's role: Attended the October 2025 ribbon-cutting and has championed the project as a model for rural energy and agriculture independence. In his words: "could allow Minnesota farmers to take control of their own fertilizer production."

C — SF4539: Sauk Centre / TH 71 Bonding

Westrom chief-authored SF4539 (2025 session) to authorize Sauk Centre voters to raise the city's local-sales-tax-backed bonding authority from $10 million to $20 million for Trunk Highway 71 reconstruction. The tax would be extended until Sauk Centre has collected $20 million or until December 31, 2045.

TH 71 is a primary north-south corridor through Stearns County; reconstruction is a multi-year MnDOT project. The bonding expansion lets the city pursue state and local financing for the local match component — a precise statutory mechanism moved through the Capitol by Westrom.

D — Pope-Douglas Solid Waste & Beardsley Drainage

$12.833 million secured for Phase 2 of the Pope-Douglas Solid Waste Management facility (waste diversion and recycling processing upgrades).

$2 million secured for the City of Beardsley's Dry Lake drainage tile system replacement — state funding covered approximately half the project cost, keeping the remainder off local property taxes.

These bonding requests illustrate how state capital investment translates into property-tax protection for District 12 communities.

Understanding Green Ammonia Economics

How the Numbers Work

Conventional anhydrous ammonia price tracks natural gas via the Henry Hub benchmark. When gas doubles, nitrogen fertilizer follows within months. Farmers in Big Stone, Stevens, and Swift counties have no control over this price mechanism.

Green ammonia replaces natural gas with electricity for the hydrogen step. The Haber-Bosch synthesis remains the same. The critical input becomes the local electricity rate, not a global commodity.

Capital estimates are illustrative, based on published research ranges. Not vendor quotes. Energy intensity: 9–10 MWh per metric ton NH₃ (Haber-Bosch electrolysis efficiency, per UMN-Morris research).

Key Variables to Watch

  • Off-peak electricity rate: the single biggest lever. Below $0.05/kWh, green ammonia becomes competitive at cooperative scale.
  • Capacity utilization: running 80%+ matters. Downtime on a capital-intensive system destroys the per-unit economics.
  • Natural gas volatility: the higher conventional ammonia goes, the better green ammonia looks relative to it.

About This Tool

Nitrogen fertilizer is the single largest variable cost in corn production — and its price is set by natural gas markets, not by anything a Minnesota farmer controls.

This dashboard helps you calculate what nitrogen actually costs your operation, compare it against locally-produced green ammonia, and understand what Senator Westrom's advocacy at the University of Minnesota-Morris could mean for your farm's input economics.

How to Use

  • Input Costs tab: Enter your farm's acreage, nitrogen source and price, application method, and yield goal. Results update live in the right and lower panels.
  • Green Ammonia tab: Compare your conventional nitrogen cost against a modular green ammonia production scenario. Adjust scale tier, financing, and electricity rate.
  • District 12 tab: Reference materials on the UMN-Morris green ammonia project, Westrom's legislative wins, and key resources for farmers.
Data persistence: Your inputs are saved in your browser's local storage. They will persist when you reload this page. Use the "Clear" button to reset to demo data.
Print: Use your browser's print function (Ctrl+P) to generate a clean printout of all modules suitable for a co-op board meeting or extension office visit.
