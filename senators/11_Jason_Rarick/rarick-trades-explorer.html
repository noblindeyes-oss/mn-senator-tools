    Preparation Tips

    Most apprenticeship programs require a math aptitude test covering algebra and basic arithmetic. Khan Academy offers free, self-paced algebra courses at khanacademy.org/math/algebra. Focus on solving for unknowns, working with fractions, and reading formulas — the kinds of math you use to calculate voltage drop and load calculations on the job.
    Apprentices are expected to arrive job-site ready. Before your first day, acquire: steel-toed work boots (leather, ANSI-rated), safety glasses, a hard hat (class E for electrical work), work gloves, and a basic hand tool set (voltage tester, pliers, screwdrivers, wire strippers). Many JATCs provide a tool list; ask during orientation. Budget $200–$400 for initial gear.
    An informational interview with a working tradesperson is the most accurate way to understand day-to-day reality — what a job site actually looks like, how physical the work is, what they wish they had known before applying. Contact your local union hall or ask a high school counselor to connect you with a tradesperson willing to talk. Most are glad to help.
    Pine Technical & Community College in Pine City offers pre-apprenticeship electrical courses through its Electrical Technology program. Completing a semester of coursework there can strengthen your JATC application and give you a head start on the classroom portion of apprenticeship training. It also confirms the trade is a fit before committing to a 5-year program. Visit pine.edu for current course offerings.

    The Rarick Pathway — A Worked Example

    One Path. Real Numbers. No Debt.

    A Pine City High School graduate enters an apprenticeship program in 1992. Thirty years later he chairs the Minnesota Senate committee that sets policy for the same system he came up through. This is what that path actually looked like.

    1 / 7

    This is one path. The point isn't that everyone should become a Master Electrician or a state senator. The point is that a 5-year apprenticeship can be the foundation for a stable career, a business, or something else entirely — without the debt. In 2025, Minnesota is projected to need thousands of additional licensed electricians, plumbers, and HVAC technicians. The pipeline needs people. This tool is for them.

    Senator Rarick entered IBEW Local 110 as an apprentice electrician in 1992 — before he ever thought about running for office. Thirty years later he chairs the committee that sets policy for the same trades pipeline he came up through. This tool was built because that pipeline still needs people, and the pathway is not as well-understood as it should be.
