"Does This Apply to Me?" — A Compliance Reference for Officials in Minnesota's Classified Cities
Senator Ann Rest introduced SF2694 in the 2025 session to extend Minnesota's Statement of Economic Interest requirement to elected and appointed officials in cities of the first, second, and third class — the 100 largest cities in Minnesota, which include communities where Rest has worked for four decades. Under current law (Minn. Stat. § 10A.09), state officials and officials of metropolitan governmental units must file with the Campaign Finance and Public Disclosure Board. SF2694 would bring the same standard to major-city officials statewide. This reference tool explains what that requirement means in practice.

§ 2. What Must Be Disclosed

Understanding the Statement of Economic Interest

The Statement of Economic Interest filed with the CFPB covers four categories. Each is explained below in plain language.

What it means. Any source that paid you more than $2,500 in the prior calendar year, other than your salary from the position you hold. This includes employers, clients, contracts, and board compensation.

What it does NOT include. Your city salary, Social Security, pension payments, or investment dividends below threshold.

Common confusion. Self-employed officials must list their clients or their business type if the business type could intersect with city decisions.

Example Scenario
A council member in a second-class city also operates a small engineering consulting firm. The firm earned fees from three clients last year. The council member must list the firm and disclose the nature of the business — not individual client names — unless a specific client paid the firm more than $2,500 for work that intersects with city business.
Red Flag Scenario
A planning commissioner receives $5,000 in consulting fees from a developer who has a pending rezoning application before the commission. The income must be disclosed. Additionally, the commissioner should evaluate whether recusal from the rezoning vote is required under separate conflict-of-interest obligations.

Consequence of non-disclosure: Referral to CFPB; civil penalty up to $1,000 per violation under Minn. Stat. § 10A.02.

What it means. Ownership interest in any business (not publicly traded stock) that is greater than a de minimis threshold. For city officials considering SF2694's scope, this matters most when the business is a vendor, developer, or contractor that does business with the city.

What it does NOT include. Publicly traded stock holdings or mutual funds.

Example Scenario
A mayor holds a 25% ownership stake in a local excavation company. The city regularly contracts with excavation firms for utility work. The mayor must disclose the ownership interest in the statement. The disclosure does not prohibit the company from bidding on city contracts — but it allows the public and the CFPB to see the potential overlap.
Red Flag Scenario
A council member owns 50% of a property management company. The city is considering a new rental licensing ordinance that would directly affect the company's cost of operations. The financial holding must be disclosed, and the council member should consider recusal from the licensing vote.

Consequence of non-disclosure: Referral to CFPB; civil penalty up to $1,000 per violation under Minn. Stat. § 10A.02.

What it means. Any real property you own other than your primary residence — rental properties, vacant land, commercial real estate, second homes.

Why it matters. City officials frequently make zoning and assessment decisions that directly affect property values. The disclosure allows the public and the CFPB to flag potential conflicts before a vote occurs.

Example Scenario
A city administrator owns a rental duplex two blocks from a proposed redevelopment site. Even though the administrator is not voting on the project, the property interest must be disclosed. If the redevelopment could materially affect the duplex's value, the administrator should also disclose the proximity to supervisors and legal counsel.
Red Flag Scenario
A zoning board member owns vacant land directly adjacent to a parcel for which a variance is being requested. The variance could significantly increase the value of the board member's land. This must be disclosed, and the board member must recuse from the variance decision.

Consequence of non-disclosure: Referral to CFPB; civil penalty up to $1,000 per violation under Minn. Stat. § 10A.02.

What it means. Any ownership or licensed interest in a horse used for racing in Minnesota, or in a racing license.

This category is a legacy provision in Minn. Stat. § 10A.09. It appears narrow but reflects Minnesota's historic pari-mutuel regulation framework. Most city officials will have nothing to report here.

Example Scenario
A council member co-owns a standardbred horse that races at Running Aces. The ownership interest must be disclosed, even if the council member's city has no direct involvement in racing regulation.
Red Flag Scenario
A city official with a racing interest votes on a land-use matter involving a racetrack facility. The racing interest must be disclosed, and the vote may trigger a conflict-of-interest review.

Consequence of non-disclosure: Referral to CFPB; civil penalty up to $1,000 per violation under Minn. Stat. § 10A.02.

Conflict of Interest vs. Disclosure

Disclosure does not prohibit a conflict — it makes it visible. Filing a Statement of Economic Interest tells the public what financial interests you hold. The separate obligation to recuse — to abstain from voting where you have a direct financial interest in the outcome — is a different legal requirement. Both work together: disclosure identifies potential conflicts; recusal prevents those conflicts from influencing decisions. An official who discloses properly but fails to recuse when required, or who recuses but fails to disclose, has met only half the obligation.

Recusal obligations for city officials arise under Minn. Stat. § 471.87 and § 471.88, in addition to any local ethics ordinance.

Minnesota classifies cities by population under Minn. Stat. § 410.01. First-class cities have populations over 100,000 (Minneapolis, St. Paul, Rochester, and Duluth). Second-class cities have populations between 20,001 and 100,000. Third-class cities have populations between 10,001 and 20,000. Once classified as first class, a city keeps that status unless its population falls more than 25 percent below the qualifying threshold.
