The Problem

We are trying to treat a condition without a diagnostic panel.

Senator Mann's SF513 proposes something precise: a state office with a director, annual reports, and a mandate to collect data that Minnesota doesn't currently have in one place. This tool was built to show what that office would actually use — and what the state is missing without it.

Minnesota's gun violence data is fragmented across four separate systems. Different scopes. Different latencies. Different methodologies. No single system captures both fatal and nonfatal firearm injuries with consistent mechanism coding statewide.

Module 1 — Data Landscape

Minnesota's Four Data Systems

Minnesota Violent Death Reporting System (MNVDRS)

MN Department of Health, Injury & Violence Prevention
Captures
Firearm deaths — homicide, suicide, legal intervention, unintentional, undetermined intent
Scope
Statewide; county-level breakdowns available
Latency
12-18 months behind current year
Limitation
Deaths only. Does not capture nonfatal firearm injuries, which outnumber deaths roughly 2:1 nationally. Does not capture incidents without ER contact.
Methodology
Three-source triangulation: death certificates + medical examiner records + law enforcement records (gold standard)

MDH Injury Data / MIDAS

MN Department of Health — Minnesota Interactive Data Access System
Captures
Emergency department visits and hospitalizations, including some firearm-mechanism codes; also tracks fatal injury by mechanism
Scope
Statewide; hospital-level discharge data
Latency
12-24 months
Limitation
Requires ICD-10 external cause codes (W32-W34, X72-X74, X93-X95, Y22-Y24, Y35.0). Not all hospitals code consistently. Does not capture incidents where person did not seek care or died before hospital contact.

MDH Syndromic Surveillance

MN Department of Health
Captures
Real-time ED chief complaints; can flag injury patterns
Scope
Participating hospitals (not 100% coverage)
Latency
Near real-time (24-72 hours)
Limitation
Designed for outbreak detection — very blunt on mechanism. Does not reliably distinguish firearm injuries from other penetrating trauma. No case-level detail. No follow-up.

CDC WISQARS

U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention — Web-based Injury Statistics Query and Reporting System
Captures
Fatal and nonfatal injury data; firearm deaths and injuries
Scope
National, with state-level breakdowns
Latency
1-3 years depending on data type
Limitation
MN-specific data has wide confidence intervals at county level due to small numbers. National tool not designed for state-level program management. Does not integrate with MN-specific prevention programs.

Coverage Gap Summary

  • No single system captures both fatal and nonfatal firearm injuries with consistent mechanism coding statewide.
  • Nonfatal injuries — estimated at 2x the number of deaths — are the largest data gap in current Minnesota surveillance.
  • A dedicated Office of Gun Violence Prevention would require a unified reporting protocol linking MNVDRS, MIDAS, and law-enforcement incident data under a single annual data release.

District Context

SD-50: Edina and Southwest First-Ring Suburbs

Population ~53,494 · Median household income ~$91,847 · Median age 45.2 · 24.2% under 18 · 20.7% over 65

An affluent, aging suburb with a significant pediatric and elderly population. The suicide rate in high-income older suburban populations is often above average. The safe-storage-for-children message resonates in an upper-middle-class family district. District 50 residents experience gun violence through a different lens than urban core or rural populations — the prevention frame (safe storage, crisis intervention, data-driven targeting) fits this constituency.
