Methodology

All projections use a discrete annual logistic growth model: N(t+1) = N(t) + r*N(t)*(1 - N(t)/K) - H - M*N(t). Population is floored at zero and capped at 1.05*K to allow slight overshoot before density-dependent correction. Equilibrium estimates are derived by running the simulation forward 50 years and reporting the final value. Maximum Sustainable Yield (MSY) is calculated as MSY = r*K/4, occurring at population size N = K/2 — the classic result from the logistic growth model.

Conflict risk is assessed qualitatively based on year-10 population density relative to carrying capacity: above 80% of K is rated High (increased competition for territory, livestock depredation, vehicle strikes), 50-80% is Medium, and below 50% is Low. Economic activity estimates are per-unit multipliers (deer: ~$1,200/unit, walleye: ~$45/fish, goose: ~$85/bird). Wolf harvest has no commercial value; its management rationale is ecological balance and conflict reduction.

Senator Wesenberg spent approximately 20 years as a DNR wildlife biologist at Camp Ripley, tracking wolves with GPS, studying bears, eagles, deer, and turtles. District Context

SD-10: Where Science Meets the Landscape

SD-10 covers Morrison, Aitkin, and parts of Mille Lacs, Kanabec, and Benton counties — central Minnesota lake-and-river country with a tourism and outdoor-recreation economy. Camp Ripley in Morrison County is both a National Guard training facility and a site with significant wildlife research history; Wesenberg's DNR work was based there for roughly 20 years. Little Falls is the Morrison County seat and Wesenberg's hometown.

In the 2026 session, Wesenberg co-authored SF3773 to appropriate $500,000 from the Environment and Natural Resources Trust Fund to replace the Sullivan Lake Dam in Morrison County — a concrete example of using conservation trust mechanisms to fund infrastructure that protects lake ecosystems. Sullivan Lake is a Morrison County water body where dam failure or deterioration affects water levels, fish habitat, and downstream flooding.

Wesenberg has also offered amendments to protect forest road access for commercial minnow trappers, leech trappers, and fur trappers — the working-class outdoor economy that sustains many families in his district. Deer, walleye, wolf, and minnow issues are not abstract here. Mille Lacs walleye management has been one of the most contentious fisheries disputes in Minnesota history, and the Garrison/Mille Lacs shoreline falls within SD-10.
