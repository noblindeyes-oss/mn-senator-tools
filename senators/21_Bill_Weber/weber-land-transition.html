What Happens to the Land?
A decision guide for southwest Minnesota farm families, beginning farmers, and rural landowners in Senate District 21.

Southwest Minnesota Farm Land Transition Navigator

Built for District 21

Southwest Minnesota's farm families face the same question every generation: what happens to the land? With farmland averaging $7,000 an acre, the decision to sell, lease, conserve, or transfer a farm is not just a family matter — it shapes the housing supply, the beginning farmer pipeline, and the tax base of every small town in the region.

This guide was built for District 21 landowners navigating those choices — covering Cottonwood, Jackson, Lincoln, Lyon, Murray, Nobles, Pipestone, Redwood, Rock, and Watonwan counties.

Built to support the work of Sen. Bill Weber (SD-21), Agriculture, Rural Development & Housing Policy Committee Chair. 14 years in the Minnesota Senate. Licensed real estate broker and certified appraiser.

Section 01

The Five Paths — Farmland Transition Options

A
Outright Sale
Capital Gains Tax Beginning Farmer Credit Eligible Complexity: Moderate

Capital gains exposure. Selling farmland during life triggers federal and Minnesota state capital gains tax on the difference between the sale price and the owner's adjusted basis. The step-up in basis available at death (Path E) is forfeited if the land is sold during life — this is the single largest tax distinction between selling now and transferring at death.

Minnesota Beginning Farmer Tax Credit. Asset owners who sell to a qualified beginning farmer can claim a Minnesota state tax credit of 5% of the sale price, up to a maximum credit of $50,000. This is a direct dollar-for-dollar tax credit, not a deduction.

Installment sale alternative. Spreading the sale over multiple years through an installment agreement allows the seller to recognize gain over time, potentially keeping taxable income in a lower bracket each year.

Who regulates/assists: MN Dept. of Agriculture (beginning farmer credit) • County assessor (current estimated market value) • FSA (transition assistance)

B
Cash Rent Lease
Ordinary Income Green Acres Eligible Complexity: Low

Income treatment. Cash rent income is taxed as ordinary income, not capital gains. The landowner retains ownership and the land remains in agricultural use.

Green Acres and Rural Preserve. Land under a cash lease to an active farmer can maintain its Green Acres classification, deferring development-pressured valuation and protecting against property tax spikes. Rural Preserve offers similar protection for qualifying rural property without an agricultural income requirement.

Beginning Farmer Tax Credit. Landlords who rent to a qualified beginning farmer can claim a Minnesota state tax credit of 5% of cash rent, up to $7,000 per year.

Illustrative cash rent range. Southwest Minnesota cash rent rates vary by county and soil quality. Recent USDA NASS county averages have ranged roughly $185–$200/acre for top-tier cropland in this region (illustrative — verify current rates through your county FSA office or the MN Farm Business Management program).

Who regulates/assists: MN Dept. of Agriculture • County FSA office • MN Farm Business Management program

C
Conservation Easement
Federal Tax Deduction Permanent Restriction Complexity: High

How it works. A conservation easement permanently restricts development on the land in exchange for a payment and/or tax benefits. The federal income tax deduction equals the "bargain" value — the difference between unrestricted fair market value and restricted value (subject to AGI limits under IRC §170(h)).

Property tax impact. Land under a perpetual agricultural easement may qualify for Green Acres valuation, reducing ongoing property tax burden.

Relevant programs. USDA NRCS Agricultural Conservation Easement Program – Agricultural Land Easement (ACEP-ALE) pays landowners for perpetual easements restricting non-agricultural development. MN Board of Water & Soil Resources (BWSR) Reinvest in Minnesota (RIM) easements address wetland and buffer areas.

Wind lease conflict. Southwest Minnesota has active wind energy lease pressure. Conservation easements and wind turbine leases can conflict — a perpetual conservation restriction may prohibit turbine placement, and vice versa. Landowners considering both options should consult legal counsel before committing to either.

Who regulates/assists: NRCS • BWSR • Land Trust Alliance • County assessor

D
Wind Energy Lease
Ordinary Income 25–40 Year Term Complexity: Moderate

Why it matters here. Southwest Minnesota is one of the highest-density wind corridors in the state. Wind energy leases are a real and growing option for District 21 landowners.

Illustrative lease income. Wind lease payments typically range $4,000–$8,000 per turbine per year (illustrative — actual terms depend on the individual energy company contract). This income is taxed as ordinary income.

Agricultural classification. Wind leases do not remove land from agricultural classification for Green Acres purposes if farming continues on the remaining acreage.

Policy context. Sen. Weber's Natural Gas Innovation Act (SF421, signed June 26, 2021) expanded the policy environment for renewable energy integration, allowing Minnesota natural gas utilities to offer customers renewable natural gas, biogas, hydrogen, and ammonia alternatives.

Estate planning consideration. Wind leases typically run 25–40 years and encumber the title. This long commitment can complicate estate transfer, sale, and easement options. Plan accordingly.

Who regulates/assists: MN Public Utilities Commission • County planning/zoning • Individual energy company contracts

E
Estate Transfer / Inheritance
Step-Up in Basis MN Estate Tax Applies Complexity: High

Federal estate tax. The federal estate tax exemption is $13.61 million per individual (2024 level). Action required: this exemption is scheduled to drop to approximately $7 million in 2026 under current federal law unless Congress acts. Estates between $7M and $13.61M face a planning deadline.

Minnesota estate tax. Minnesota imposes its own estate tax with a $3 million exemption — far lower than the federal threshold. Rates range from 13% to 16% on amounts above the exemption (see the calculator in Section 3).

Weber's SF2253 (2025). This bill would increase the Minnesota estate tax general subtraction and raise the combined cap on qualified farm property and qualified small business property subtractions to $5,000,000 (current cap: $2,000,000). This change could significantly reduce or eliminate Minnesota estate tax for many farm families. Use the calculator below to see the difference for your situation.

Step-up in basis. Heirs who inherit land receive a stepped-up basis equal to fair market value at date of death. This is why timing matters: a $7,000/acre farm purchased decades ago at $1,000/acre would owe capital gains on $6,000/acre if sold during life, but the gain resets to zero at inheritance.

Green Acres trap. Deferred taxes under the Green Acres program become due and payable upon transfer if the land exits agricultural use within the three-year look-back period. Heirs who plan to develop or sell inherited farmland for non-agricultural purposes should account for this recapture.

Conservation easements before death. Placing a conservation easement on land before death reduces the estate's taxable value, potentially bringing it below the MN exemption threshold.

Who regulates/assists: MN Dept. of Revenue • Estate attorney • Certified appraiser
