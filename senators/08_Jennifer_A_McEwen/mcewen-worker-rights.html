Minnesota's 2023 labor session was the most consequential for workers in a generation. Every Minnesota worker — full-time, part-time, gig — now has the right to earned sick and safe time. Non-compete clauses signed after July 1, 2023 are void. Employers can no longer force workers to sit through anti-union meetings. These rights exist. This tool helps you use them.

The Minnesota Legislature's 2023 labor session — led in the Senate by Jennifer McEwen (SD-8) — gave every Minnesota worker the right to earned sick and safe time, voided non-compete agreements, and banned captive audience anti-union meetings. This tool was built to help workers in Senate District 8 and across Minnesota actually use those rights.

SECTION 02

Law Reference

What the law actually says — plain language summaries with expandable key rules and exceptions

Earned Sick and Safe Time (ESST)

Minn. Stat. § 181.9445–181.9448
Starting January 1, 2024, every employee working in Minnesota — full-time, part-time, or seasonal — earns paid sick and safe leave automatically. For every 30 hours you work, you earn 1 hour of paid leave, up to 48 hours per year. You can use this time when you're sick, when a family member is sick, or when you need safety-related leave (such as for domestic violence or stalking situations). Your employer cannot punish you for using it.

Key Rules

  • Coverage: All employees working in Minnesota for any employer with 1 or more employees. This includes full-time, part-time, and temporary workers.
  • Accrual rate: 1 hour of ESST for every 30 hours worked. Accrual begins from your first day of work — not after any waiting period.
  • Annual cap: Employers may cap accrual at 48 hours per year.
  • Carryover: Up to 80 hours of unused ESST may carry over to the next year. Employers may cap use at 48 hours in a calendar year.
  • 90-day rule (use only): Employers may require new employees to wait up to 90 days before they can first USE accrued ESST. Accrual still begins on day one — the waiting period only affects when you can take the leave.
  • Permitted uses: Your own illness, injury, or health condition; care for a family member's illness; domestic violence, sexual assault, or stalking (safety leave); school or childcare closures due to public health emergency.
  • Notice requirement: Employer may require up to 7 days advance notice for foreseeable leave; "reasonable" notice for unforeseeable leave (e.g., sudden illness). Employer cannot require more than this.
  • Balance visibility: Employers must provide access to your ESST balance — typically on your pay stub or via an online portal.
  • Retaliation protection: It is illegal for your employer to discipline, discharge, dock pay, or otherwise penalize you for using ESST.
  • Enforcement: Complaints filed with the Minnesota Department of Labor and Industry (DLI). Workers may also have a private right of action.
DULUTH NOTE: Duluth enacted its own earned sick and safe time ordinance in 2016 — one of the first cities in Minnesota to do so. If you work within Duluth city limits, you are covered by both the state law and the city ordinance. You get whichever provides more generous protections. Contact the Duluth Human Rights Office for city-ordinance complaints.

Exceptions & Edge Cases

  • Independent contractors: The ESST law covers employees, not true independent contractors. However, worker misclassification is a widespread practice — if you work like an employee (set schedule, employer direction, single employer) but are paid as a 1099 contractor, you may actually be entitled to ESST. This is a gray area worth discussing with an attorney or DLI.
  • Existing PTO policies: If your employer has an existing paid time off (PTO) policy that meets or exceeds the ESST requirements, it may satisfy the law — but only if the accrual, usage, and retaliation protections are equivalent.
  • Collective bargaining: Certain provisions can be waived by a collective bargaining agreement (CBA) if the waiver is explicit and the CBA provides equivalent protections.
  • Employer notice posting: Employers are required to post notice of ESST rights. If your workplace has no posting, that itself may be worth reporting to DLI.

Non-Compete Agreement Ban

Minn. Stat. § 181.988
Effective July 1, 2023, any non-compete agreement signed on or after that date is void and unenforceable in Minnesota. If you were asked to sign something saying you can't work for a competitor or start a competing business after leaving your job, and you signed it on or after July 1, 2023, that agreement cannot be used against you. It is legally null.

Key Rules

  • Controlling date: July 1, 2023. Non-compete agreements signed ON OR AFTER this date are void. Agreements signed before this date are not automatically void.
  • What is banned: Covenants not to compete — agreements that restrict an employee from working for a competitor or starting a competing business after leaving the employer.
  • Legal consequence: A void agreement is unenforceable from the start. An employer cannot sue you based on a void non-compete. If they threaten to, that threat has no legal foundation under Minnesota law.
  • Employer can still present one: An employer can still hand you a non-compete to sign — but if you sign it today, it is immediately void. You cannot be bound by it.
  • Remedy: If an employer attempts to enforce a void non-compete, the employee may seek relief including attorneys' fees.

Exceptions & Fine Print

  • NDAs still valid: Non-disclosure agreements (NDAs) that protect trade secrets and confidential information remain enforceable. The ban is specific to non-compete clauses restricting future employment or business competition.
  • Non-solicitation of customers: Certain non-solicitation clauses (prohibiting you from poaching employer customers after leaving) may still be enforceable in some circumstances — this is a gray area. Consult an attorney if your agreement includes one.
  • Trade secret protections: Federal and state trade secret laws remain in effect. Leaving an employer does not entitle you to take proprietary information.
  • Pre-July 2023 agreements: Non-competes signed before July 1, 2023 are still potentially enforceable, subject to a reasonableness analysis: courts examine the duration, geographic scope, and whether the employer has a legitimate business interest to protect. Many pre-2023 non-competes have been found unreasonable. Consult an attorney if you are being threatened with enforcement of an older agreement.
  • Sale of business exception: Non-compete agreements entered into in connection with the sale of a business may be treated differently. Consult an attorney.

Captive Audience Meeting Ban

Minn. Stat. § 179.30
Effective May 24, 2023, Minnesota employers cannot require you to attend meetings where they express their views on unions, union organizing, political matters, or religion. If attendance is mandatory and the employer is pushing an anti-union message, that is now likely illegal. You cannot be disciplined or threatened for refusing to attend or for walking out.

Key Rules

  • What is prohibited: Employers may not require employees to attend any meeting at which the employer communicates the employer's views on political or religious matters, or on matters relating to union organizing.
  • No retaliation for refusal: An employer cannot discipline, discharge, threaten, or penalize an employee for refusing to attend, for leaving, or for failing to participate in such a meeting.
  • Private right of action: Employees may file a civil lawsuit against an employer for violations. Remedies include compensatory damages and attorneys' fees.
  • Documentation matters: If you believe a meeting violated this law, document everything — the date, who called the meeting, what was said, and who attended.

Exceptions & Fine Print

  • Voluntary meetings: Meetings that are genuinely voluntary — where there is truly no negative consequence for not attending — are not prohibited. The issue is when attendance is coerced through scheduling, supervisor pressure, or threat of adverse action.
  • Informational / operational meetings: Meetings that are genuinely educational or informational about operations, benefits, or workplace policies are not banned — even if they touch on related subjects — provided they do not include the employer's views on union organizing or political/religious matters.
  • "Optional" with pressure: If a meeting is labeled optional but supervisors track attendance, or if there is an implicit expectation, courts may still find it constitutes a captive audience situation. Context matters.
  • Contested provision: This was among the most heavily contested parts of the 2023 labor package and drew significant employer opposition. Expect that employers aware of the law will carefully label meetings as "voluntary" — the substance and context determine compliance, not just the label.

Union Organizing Rights

NLRA + 2023 MN Enhancements
The right to organize, join, and form unions is primarily protected by the federal National Labor Relations Act (NLRA). Minnesota's 2023 labor package strengthened state-level protections in several areas — making it easier to join and form unions, and protecting workers during organizing campaigns. Major Duluth employers like Essentia Health, St. Luke's, UMD, and the Twin Ports trades operate in heavily unionized sectors with robust protections.

Key Rules (Federal NLRA Framework)

  • Right to organize: Private-sector employees have the right to form, join, or assist labor unions and to engage in collective bargaining through representatives of their own choosing.
  • Right to concerted activity: Workers have the right to act together for mutual aid or protection — including discussing wages, working conditions, and grievances — even outside of a formal union context.
  • Protection from interference: Employers may not interfere with, restrain, or coerce employees exercising these rights. This includes threatening job loss, cutting hours, or making promises to discourage organizing.
  • 2023 MN enhancements: The 2023 package expanded state-level organizing rights and protections in several areas. For specifics about how these apply in your sector, contact the Minnesota AFL-CIO, your union if you have one, or consult a labor attorney. (This tool does not cite specific 2023 MN statute numbers for provisions not independently verifiable.)
  • Public sector: Most state and local government employees are covered by Minnesota's Public Employment Labor Relations Act (PELRA) rather than the NLRA — similar framework, different agency.

Duluth Sector Notes

  • Healthcare (Essentia, St. Luke's): SEIU and other healthcare unions are active in Duluth's hospital sector. NLRA applies to most private hospital employees.
  • Trades / Ports (USW, Teamsters, Building Trades): Strong historical union presence in Twin Ports shipping, steelworking, and construction. Collective bargaining agreements govern most working conditions.
  • Education (DPS, UMD): Public school teachers and UMD employees are covered by PELRA. AFSCME is active in public sector Duluth employment.
  • Gig/app-based workers: Current federal law generally does not extend NLRA protections to independent contractors. The misclassification question (are you really an employee?) is critical here.

Duluth Local Sick Leave Ordinance

Duluth City Code (2016)
Duluth adopted its own earned sick and safe time ordinance in 2016 — one of the earliest in Minnesota, predating the state law by years. If you work within Duluth city limits, you are covered by both the state ESST law and the Duluth ordinance. You receive whichever set of protections is more generous.

Key Details

  • Coverage: Employees working within Duluth city limits, for employers with 5 or more employees (smaller threshold considerations may apply — check with the Human Rights Office).
  • Consistency with state law: The Duluth ordinance is broadly consistent with the 2024 state ESST law. In most cases the state law now provides at least as generous protections — but the city ordinance may offer additional local enforcement mechanisms.
  • Local enforcement: Complaints about violations of the Duluth ordinance can be filed with the Duluth Human Rights Office. This is separate from DLI's state enforcement.
  • "More generous" rule: If you are covered by both, you receive the benefit of whichever protection is broader in any given area. You do not lose city protections because a state law now exists.

Next Steps & Resources

Real organizations that can help — grouped by issue type
Earned Sick and Safe Time Complaints
Minnesota Department of Labor and Industry (DLI)
dli.mn.gov
Phone: 651-284-5005
The state agency that enforces the ESST law. You can file a complaint online or by phone. DLI investigates claims that an employer failed to provide ESST, retaliated against a worker for using ESST, or failed to track and disclose balances.
Bring to complaint: Your pay stubs showing hours worked, any written communication from your employer about sick leave, dates you were denied leave or disciplined, and the name and address of your employer.
City of Duluth Human Rights Office
duluthmn.gov/human-rights-office
Enforces Duluth's local earned sick and safe time ordinance for employers operating within Duluth city limits. For workers in Duluth, this is a parallel enforcement option — you may file with the city office, DLI, or both.
Bring to complaint: Same as DLI — pay stubs, communications from employer, dates and specifics of the violation.
Non-Compete and Labor Law Issues
Minnesota Department of Labor and Industry — Labor Standards
dli.mn.gov/business/employment-practices/labor-standards
DLI's Labor Standards division handles a broad range of employment law questions including wage theft, misclassification, and general compliance. For non-compete questions, they can provide guidance on the 2023 law even if formal complaint mechanisms differ.
Legal Aid Service of Northeastern Minnesota
legalaidmn.org
Serves Duluth and St. Louis County. Provides free civil legal assistance to income-qualifying workers. Can help with non-compete questions, wage theft, wrongful termination, and related employment law issues. This is the first call for a Duluth worker who needs an attorney but cannot afford one.
Bring to intake: Your employment contract or offer letter, the non-compete agreement if you have one, any communications from your employer about the agreement, and documentation of any threats or legal action.
Union and Organizing Questions
Minnesota AFL-CIO
mnaflcio.org
The state federation of labor unions. Can connect workers with organizing resources, refer to appropriate affiliated unions, and provide information about worker rights under the 2023 labor package. Active in labor education statewide.
NLRB Region 18 — Minneapolis
nlrb.gov — Region 18
The regional office of the National Labor Relations Board covering Minnesota. Handles charges related to unfair labor practices under federal law — including employer interference with organizing, retaliation for union activity, and refusal to bargain. For captive audience meeting violations that may also implicate federal law, this is a parallel avenue to the state statute.
Bring to complaint: Dates and details of the alleged unfair labor practice, names of witnesses, any written communications, and your employer's legal name and address.
General Worker Rights
Minnesota Attorney General's Office — Wage Theft Unit
ag.state.mn.us/Consumer/WorkplaceRights
The MN AG's office has authority over wage theft and worker exploitation. If your employer has been withholding earned wages, misclassifying workers, or engaging in systematic violation of labor laws, this office can investigate and take action on behalf of affected workers — not just individuals.
TakeAction Minnesota
takeactionminnesota.org
A community organizing and civic power-building organization active statewide. Engages workers on labor rights, economic justice, and civic engagement. Can connect workers to community organizing efforts and advocacy resources beyond formal legal channels.
OutFront Minnesota
outfront.org
Statewide LGBTQ+ advocacy organization. Relevant for workers navigating intersecting issues — workplace discrimination alongside labor rights questions. Can connect workers to LGBTQ+-competent legal resources.
