District 62 is one of the most renter-dense areas in Minnesota. In Cedar-Riverside, nearly 9 in 10 households rent. In 2023 and 2024, the Minnesota Legislature passed the most significant new tenant protections in state history — including new rules on eviction notices, late fees, landlord entry, and the right to organize.

Senator Fateh’s work in the Legislature helped pass these protections. This tool was built to make them legible to the people they were written for — the renters of District 62. In 2024, nearly 20,000 Minnesota renter households had to call a hotline just to learn what they were already owed by law. This tool is built so you can find those answers without waiting on hold.

    Situation A: Eviction

    Question 1 of 2

    Did you receive written notice from your landlord before they filed for eviction?

    Your Rights Have Been Violated

    As of January 1, 2024, Minnesota law requires your landlord to give you a written 14-day notice before filing an eviction for nonpayment of rent. If no written notice was given, the eviction filing may be improper and you may be able to have it dismissed.

    Minn. Stat. §504B.321, effective January 1, 2024

    What To Do Next

    1. Write down the date you received any communication from your landlord and how it was delivered (text, verbal, posted on door, etc.).
    2. If you have a court date, attend it and tell the judge you did not receive the required 14-day written notice.
    3. Bring any text messages, emails, or notes showing the landlord did not give written notice.
    4. Call HOME Line for free advice before your court date.
    HOME Line: 612-728-5767 En Español: 612-255-8870
    Follow-up Question

    When did you receive the written notice?

    --
    Days remaining in your 14-day notice period

    Your landlord cannot file for eviction until the 14-day notice period expires. If they file before this date, the filing may be improper.

    You are still within your 14-day notice period. Use this time to: gather any documents showing you owe rent or disputes about the amount, explore emergency rental assistance, and contact HOME Line for advice on your options.

    Minn. Stat. §504B.321 — 14-day pre-eviction notice requirement

    What To Do Now

    1. Contact your landlord in writing (text or email) to document your response.
    2. Apply for emergency rental assistance if you owe back rent: Hennepin County Emergency Assistance 612-696-0804.
    3. Call HOME Line for free advice: 612-728-5767.

    Your 14-day notice period has passed. If an eviction has been filed, you will receive a court summons. Prepare for your hearing using the checklist below.

    Court Date Preparation Checklist
    • Bring your lease agreement
    • Bring all rent receipts or bank records showing payments made
    • Bring the original written notice from your landlord
    • Bring any written communication with your landlord (texts, emails)
    • Arrive at least 30 minutes before your hearing time
    • Ask about available interpreters if you need one (request in advance if possible)
    • Contact Legal Aid Society of Minneapolis if you need free legal help: 612-334-5970

    Free Help Available

    1. Call HOME Line for pre-hearing advice: 612-728-5767
    2. Call Legal Aid Society: 612-334-5970
    Question 2 of 2

    What is the reason given for the eviction?

    Nonpayment evictions: Your landlord must have given you the 14-day written notice (see above). You have the right to pay the full amount owed at any time before a court judgment is entered to stop the eviction. Keep records of any payment you make.

    Minn. Stat. §504B.321 — right to cure nonpayment before judgment

    Lease violation evictions: Your landlord must specify the exact lease provision you violated. You have the right to know what the violation is. In some cases you may be able to cure the violation (fix the problem) before a judgment is entered.

    End of tenancy: If your lease has expired or your landlord is not renewing, they must still follow proper notice requirements. Month-to-month tenants are typically entitled to advance notice before being required to leave. Check your lease for the required notice period.

    Situation B: Unexpected Fee

    Minnesota law restricts what fees your landlord can charge you and requires disclosure of all non-optional fees before you sign your lease.

    What type of fee was charged?

    Late fees are capped by law. Your landlord can only charge a late fee if: (1) it is agreed to in writing in your lease, and (2) the fee is no more than 8% of the overdue rent amount. They cannot charge a flat late fee that exceeds this cap.

    Example: If you owe $1,000 in rent, the maximum late fee is $80.

    Minn. Stat. §504B.177 — late fee cap at 8% of overdue rent

    What To Do Next

    1. Check your lease to see if a late fee is included in writing.
    2. Calculate 8% of your overdue rent amount.
    3. If the fee exceeds 8%, you can dispute it in writing to your landlord.
    4. Document everything in writing (email or text).

    Pet fees: Landlords may charge a pet deposit or pet fee for non-service, non-emotional-support animals if it is agreed to in writing in your lease. However, this fee must be disclosed before you sign your lease. An undisclosed pet fee added after signing may be challenged.

    What To Do Next

    1. Check if the pet fee was disclosed in your lease before you signed.
    2. If it was not disclosed before signing, dispute it in writing.
    This Fee is Illegal

    A landlord cannot charge any fee for a service animal or emotional support animal. This is prohibited under Minnesota law. It does not matter what your lease says — a lease provision requiring a fee for a service or support animal is unenforceable.

    Minn. Stat. §504B.113 (as amended) — no fees for service/support animals

    What To Do Next

    1. Demand a refund of the fee in writing immediately.
    2. If your landlord refuses, contact HOME Line: 612-728-5767.
    3. You may also file a complaint with the Minnesota Department of Human Rights.

    Move-in and application fees must be disclosed before you agree to rent. If a fee was not disclosed before you signed your lease or paid your deposit, you may be able to challenge it. Keep records of all fees paid and when they were disclosed to you.

    Month-to-month fees: Some leases include a premium for month-to-month tenancy. This must be disclosed in writing before you sign your lease. If this fee appeared after you signed, or was not disclosed, dispute it in writing.

    Undisclosed Fees May Be Unenforceable

    Your landlord must disclose all non-optional fees before you sign your lease. If a mandatory fee was added after you signed — or was never disclosed — you have grounds to dispute it. This includes administrative fees, utility management fees, or any fee not in your original lease.

    What To Do Next

    1. Review your original lease carefully for every fee listed.
    2. Write down the fee charged, the date, and how it was communicated to you.
    3. Send a written dispute to your landlord asking for the legal basis for the fee.
    4. If unresolved: HOME Line 612-728-5767.

    Situation C: Landlord Entered Without Notice

    Minnesota Law Requires Advance Notice

    Your landlord must give you at least 24 hours advance notice before entering your unit. The only exception is a genuine emergency (such as a fire, flood, or burst pipe). Routine inspections, repairs, or showings to prospective tenants all require 24-hour notice.

    Minn. Stat. §504B.211 — 24-hour advance notice before landlord entry

    If your landlord enters without notice: you have the right to demand this not happen again, in writing. Repeated unauthorized entry may constitute harassment or an interference with your right to quiet enjoyment — which can support a legal complaint.

    What To Do Next

    1. Write down the date and time of the unauthorized entry, and what your landlord did while in your unit.
    2. Send a written notice to your landlord reminding them of the 24-hour notice requirement.
    3. If unauthorized entry continues, contact HOME Line: 612-728-5767.
    4. You may also file a complaint with the city of Minneapolis.
    HOME Line: 612-728-5767

    Situation D: Rental Application Rejected

    Question 1 of 2

    Is the rental unit located within the City of Minneapolis?

    Minneapolis Has Stronger Protections

    In the City of Minneapolis, a landlord cannot reject you based on any one of the following alone: your credit score, your criminal history, lack of credit history, or prior eviction history. If you have a consistent record of paying rent on time, you can provide that as evidence of your ability to pay — even if you don’t meet the stated income threshold or don’t have a traditional credit history.

    Statewide: Your landlord must provide you a written adverse action notice explaining why you were denied and what screening criteria were used. They must also disclose their screening criteria upfront. If you were charged a screening fee, they must return it if they do not run a screen.

    Question 2 of 2

    Were you denied because of your immigration status or because you don’t have a Social Security number?

    This May Be an Illegal Rejection

    Minnesota’s 2024 tenant protection law prohibits denying a rental application based on immigration status. Landlords also cannot require a Social Security number as the sole form of ID — other valid government identification must be accepted. If you were rejected for either of these reasons, you may have a legal claim.

    What To Do Next

    1. Get the denial in writing if you don’t have it already.
    2. Contact HOME Line: 612-728-5767.
    3. Contact the Minnesota Department of Human Rights to file a complaint.
    4. Contact Legal Aid Society: 612-334-5970.
    HOME Line: 612-728-5767

    What To Do Next

    1. Request the written adverse action notice from the landlord if you haven’t received one.
    2. Review what screening criteria they used and whether those criteria were disclosed upfront.
    3. Call HOME Line for advice on challenging the denial: 612-728-5767.

    Situation E: Landlord Retaliation

    You Have the Right to Organize

    Minnesota law protects tenants who organize with their neighbors, join a tenant union, report housing code violations, contact the media, or file complaints about their building. Your landlord cannot evict you, raise your rent, reduce your services, or threaten you because of these protected activities. This protection applies even if you are in a dispute with your landlord about other issues.

    Minn. Stat. §504B.285 (as amended) — prohibition on landlord retaliation

    Protected actions include: organizing meetings with other tenants, joining or forming a tenant union, contacting local media, filing complaints with the city or county, reporting code violations to housing inspectors, and requesting repairs in writing.

    Signs of retaliation: eviction notice shortly after you exercised a protected right, sudden rent increase, reduction in building services, harassment, or refusal to make repairs that were previously requested.

    What To Do Next

    1. Document the timeline: write down the date you exercised your protected right, and the date your landlord took the adverse action.
    2. Keep copies of all written communication with your landlord.
    3. Contact Inquilinxs Unidxs por Justicia (tenant union organizing): inquilinxsunidxs.org
    4. Contact HOME Line: 612-728-5767.
    5. Contact Legal Aid Society if you are facing eviction as retaliation: 612-334-5970.
    HOME Line: 612-728-5767

    Situation F: Repair Problem

    Your Landlord Must Maintain Habitability

    Your landlord is required by law to maintain your unit in a habitable condition. This includes working heat, plumbing, structural integrity, and freedom from pest infestations. A landlord who fails to make necessary repairs after written notice may be subject to a rent escrow proceeding — where you pay rent to the court instead of the landlord until repairs are made.

    The legal repair process:

    1. Step 1 — Written notice: Send a written request to your landlord describing the repair needed, the date it started, and the impact on your use of the unit. Keep a copy.
    2. Step 2 — Wait for response: For urgent repairs (no heat in winter, sewage backup), your landlord must respond promptly — often within 24 hours. For non-urgent repairs, a reasonable time is 14 days.
    3. Step 3 — If no response: You can file a rent escrow petition with Housing Court. You deposit your rent with the court, and the judge can order the landlord to make repairs or reduce your rent.

    What To Do Next

    1. Send a written repair request today — by email, text, or certified mail. Date and describe the problem clearly.
    2. Take photos and video of the problem and save them dated.
    3. Do NOT withhold rent without going through the formal rent escrow process — contact HOME Line first.
    4. If the landlord does not respond: HOME Line 612-728-5767 or Legal Aid 612-334-5970.
