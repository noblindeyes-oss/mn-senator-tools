Can Your Community
Own Its Land?

A Feasibility and Financing Guide for Manufactured Home Park Resident Groups

Manufactured home park residents across Minnesota's north metro own their homes but rent the land beneath them. When a park sells to a developer, residents can lose everything — homes that can't easily be moved, communities that took years to build. A resident cooperative can change that. This tool helps residents of any park understand whether a cooperative purchase is financially feasible, what the process looks like, and who can help. It was built to support the work of Senate District 39.

About SF2681: Senator Kunesh's SF2681 appropriated $5 million to the Minnesota Housing Development Fund specifically for resident cooperative purchases, administered through Northcountry Cooperative Foundation. This tool was built to help the resident groups who might use that funding understand whether their community is ready — and what to do next. Minnesota had approximately 61,000 manufactured homes as of recent estimates, housing some of the state's most cost-burdened households.

ROC USA Capital

Provides acquisition financing specifically for resident cooperatives purchasing manufactured home communities. Works through a network of nonprofit affiliates. As of 2025, has helped finance over 350 resident-owned communities nationally.

Loans for housing in rural areas; cooperatives in eligible communities may qualify. Income restrictions apply. Contact your USDA Rural Development state office for current eligibility.

Step 4
Cooperative Formation Roadmap

This roadmap shows what actually happens between "residents decide to explore this" and "cooperative closes on the park purchase." Expand each stage for details on who does what, typical timelines, and key documents. The process is complex — but many communities have done it successfully with good technical assistance.

1
Organize the Resident Group 1–6 months
What
Hold community meetings; identify interested households; form a steering committee; document participation (sign-in sheets, informal pledge cards).
Who
Residents — no professional required yet. Any motivated neighbor can start this process.
Timeline
1–6 months
Key Docs
Meeting minutes; list of participating households
2
Contact a Technical Assistance Provider Start immediately
What
Reach out to Northcountry Cooperative Foundation or a ROC USA affiliate for Minnesota. They provide free or low-cost pre-development assistance, help assess feasibility, and can initiate contact with potential financing partners.
Who
Steering committee contacts the provider; provider leads feasibility work from there.
Timeline
Can begin immediately; formal TA engagement typically initiates within 2–4 weeks.
Key Docs
TA engagement letter; preliminary feasibility assessment
Contact
Northcountry Cooperative Foundation: northcountry.coop  |  ROC USA: rocusa.org
3
Get the Park Appraised and Inspected 4–8 weeks
What
Obtain an independent appraisal of the park's market value; commission a physical inspection (site, water/sewer, roads, structures) to identify deferred maintenance. These inform the purchase price negotiation.
Who
TA provider coordinates; licensed appraiser and inspector conduct the work.
Timeline
4–8 weeks; costs typically $2,000–$6,000 (sometimes covered by the TA provider).
Key Docs
Appraisal report; physical needs assessment
4
Incorporate the Cooperative 4–8 weeks
What
File articles of incorporation with the Minnesota Secretary of State as a cooperative corporation (MN Statute Chapter 308A or 308B). Draft bylaws. Open a cooperative bank account. Collect membership fees.
Who
Attorney (cooperative or community development attorney) + steering committee.
Timeline
4–8 weeks; filing fee approximately $70 with MN Secretary of State.
Key Docs
Articles of incorporation; bylaws; membership agreements
5
Negotiate with the Park Owner 30–120 days
What
Submit a letter of intent to purchase. Negotiate purchase price and terms. Park owners are NOT required to sell to residents — but Minnesota law gives you an important right of first refusal (see below).
Who
TA provider and attorney support negotiations; cooperative officers sign the letter of intent.
Timeline
30–120 days depending on owner responsiveness.
Key Docs
Letter of intent; purchase agreement
Minnesota Statute 327C.095 — Right of First Refusal

Minnesota law gives manufactured home park residents the right of first refusal when their park is offered for sale. The park owner must provide at least 45 days written notice before closing a sale to a third party, during which resident cooperatives may submit a competing offer. This is your legal window — organizing before a sale notice arrives gives you the best chance of success.

6
Secure Financing 60–120 days
What
Apply for acquisition financing through ROC USA Capital, Northcountry Cooperative Foundation, USDA, or a combination. Prepare financial projections, business plan, and membership list for the lender.
Who
TA provider leads the financing application; cooperative officers sign documents.
Timeline
60–120 days for financing commitment.
Key Docs
Loan application; business plan; projected operating budget; membership agreements showing committed lot-rent payers
7
Due Diligence and Close 30–60 days post-financing
What
Final title search, environmental review, confirmation of financing terms, cooperative membership vote to approve purchase. Then close.
Who
Attorney handles closing; cooperative board signs; lender funds.
Timeline
30–60 days after financing commitment.
Key Docs
Closing documents; deed; recorded articles of incorporation
8
Operate as a Resident-Owned Community Ongoing
What
Cooperative board assumes management. Hire or contract property management if needed. Collect lot rents. Maintain reserves. Hold annual member meetings.
Who
Elected cooperative board; members vote on major decisions.
Ongoing
Annual financial audit; board elections; capital reserve planning; annual member meetings
